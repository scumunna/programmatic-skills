# Proposal: [Client] [engagement name]

Prepared by [name], [date]. A proposal earns the work by showing we understand the problem,
have a credible plan, and are the right team, then states scope, cost, and outcomes honestly.
Lead with the client's problem, not with our capabilities.

Keep the language plain and point the reader to the client glossary that ships with this skill
for any term they may not know. State outcomes as ranges, never as guarantees. No
overpromising, every number sourced, and no em dashes in the text.

## 1. Problem

The client's situation and the gap we are here to close, in their words and their business
terms. Show we have listened. If we cannot state the problem sharply, we are not ready to
propose a solution.

- The situation today: [where the client is].
- The gap or pain: [what is not working, or the opportunity being missed].
- Why it matters now: [the cost of inaction or the reason for the timing].
- How we will know it is solved: [the outcome that closes the gap].

## 2. Approach

How we will close the gap, at the level of strategy, not a task list. Name the few moves that
matter and the reasoning behind each so the client can judge the thinking, not just the
activity.

- Strategy in one paragraph: [the core approach and why it fits this problem].
- Key moves: [the two or three plays, each with the reason it will work].
- How we measure: [the KPI and how we will prove the result, including a validation of
  incrementality where conversions are the goal. See the `reporting-by-campaign-goal` skill.]
- What we will not do: [scope we are deliberately leaving out, and why].

## 3. Why us

The reason to trust this team with this problem, backed by evidence, not adjectives. Tie each
claim to proof. Drop any claim you cannot support.

- Relevant experience: [comparable work and the result it produced].
- What is different about our approach: [the specific edge, stated plainly].
- The team: [who does the work and their relevant background].
- Proof: [results, references, or case detail. Cite the source of any number.]

## 4. Scope

Exactly what is included, what is excluded, and who does what. A clear scope prevents the
disappointment that comes from an assumed deliverable that was never agreed.

- In scope: [the deliverables, listed concretely].
- Out of scope: [what is explicitly excluded].
- Client responsibilities: [what we need from the client and by when, for example access,
  approvals, creative, budget].
- Timeline: [phases and key dates].

| Phase | What we deliver | When |
| --- | --- | --- |
| [Phase 1] | [deliverable] | [date] |
| [Phase 2] | [deliverable] | [date] |

## 5. Investment

What it costs, stated clearly, with media budget separated from fees and any technology
costs. No hidden line items. The client should be able to see exactly what they are paying for.

| Item | Basis | Cost |
| --- | --- | --- |
| [Media budget] | [the working spend] | [amount] |
| [Service fee] | [for example, percentage of spend or fixed] | [amount] |
| [Technology or data costs] | [disclosed separately] | [amount] |
| Total | | [amount] |

State the contract term, payment schedule, and what happens if scope changes.

## 6. Expected outcomes (stated as ranges)

What the client can reasonably expect, given as ranges with the assumptions behind them.
These are estimates based on the plan and on comparable work, not guarantees. State that
plainly. The actual result varies with auction prices, audience response, and market
conditions.

| Outcome | Conservative | Expected | Stretch |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| [Primary KPI] | [low] | [mid] | [high] |
| [Scale, for example reach or volume] | [low] | [mid] | [high] |
| [Efficiency, for example cost per action] | [high] | [mid] | [low] |

These ranges are estimates, not commitments. They rest on the assumptions listed with each
figure. We will report actuals against this range and explain any gap. We do not guarantee a
specific result.

## 7. Next steps

Make it easy to say yes. State the immediate actions, who owns each, and the date.

- To proceed: [the one action that starts the work, for example sign and return by date].
- What we need from you: [access, budget confirmation, approvals].
- What happens next: [kickoff, onboarding, first deliverable date].
- Point of contact: [name, role, how to reach them].
