# Playbook: over-pacing

**Symptom.** The line item is ahead of its prorated target and will exhaust its budget before the flight ends, risking a dark period with no delivery at the end.

A short dark period at the end of a flight can miss the audience when it matters and can break frequency and reach goals. Slow the line item down rather than letting it burn out.

## Ordered checks

1. **Pacing mode.** Confirm the pacing mode. ASAP spends as fast as the inventory allows (far faster than even pacing) and is the usual cause of front-loaded burn. Switch to even pacing when steady delivery across the flight is the goal. Ahead pacing spends slightly faster than even to avoid underspend, which is appropriate when the risk is under-delivery, not burnout.
2. **Daily cap.** Confirm a daily budget or impression cap is set and is consistent with finishing on the flight end date. Add or lower the cap so daily spend equals remaining budget divided by remaining days.
3. **Flight length.** Confirm the flight dates are what was intended. A flight that is shorter than planned makes correct spend look like over-pacing. Extend the flight if the short window was a mistake.
4. **Bid and inventory cost.** Automated bidding feeding on unusually cheap inventory can win far more volume than planned at low CPMs. Confirm the win mix and consider a max bid ceiling or tighter inventory targeting if cheap, low-value supply is inflating volume.
5. **Insertion order interaction.** Confirm this line item is not starving siblings by consuming the shared insertion order budget early. Rebalance priorities and budgets if one line item is dominating.

## Fix

Move to even pacing and set a daily cap first, since those two levers fix the large majority of over-pacing. Then verify the flight dates and the win mix. Recheck the pace after a full day so the new daily target has time to take effect.
